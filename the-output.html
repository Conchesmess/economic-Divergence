Who Captures the Economy?
Labor vs. Capital in America

Seventy-six years of national income data reveal a slow but unmistakable transfer of economic value — from workers to corporations.

Data: U.S. Bureau of Economic Analysis via FRED  ·  Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis

Every dollar of economic output in America gets divided up. Some goes to workers as wages and benefits. Some goes to corporations as profit. The question of how that division shifts over time is one of the most consequential — and most contested — questions in economics.

This analysis starts from a simple observation: two charts on the Federal Reserve's FRED database appear to move in opposite directions over the decades. Gross Private Saving trends steadily upward. The personal saving rate trends steadily downward. Unpacking why led to a more precise and more striking finding: as a share of the total economy, labor's piece of the pie has shrunk while corporate profits' piece has grown — and the divergence has accelerated markedly since 2008.

What follows traces that investigation, from the original charts through to the clearest expression of the underlying story.

The starting point

Two Charts Moving in Opposite Directions

The original observation was that Gross Private Saving (GPSAVE) — total saving by households and businesses, in nominal dollars — trends steeply upward over the decades, while the personal saving rate — what households save as a percentage of their disposable income — trends steeply downward.

These are not really inverses. Gross Private Saving is denominated in growing nominal dollars and includes business saving (retained corporate profits plus depreciation), which has expanded enormously. The personal saving rate is a ratio that captures only household behavior. The apparent mirror image is largely an artifact of different denominators and different scopes.

But the intuition behind the observation was sound: something is diverging. The search for the right way to see it led to factor shares of national income.

Finding the right measure

Shares of the Same Pie

The cleanest way to test whether corporations are capturing greater value from the economy than households is to measure both as a share of the same total. The BEA's National Income and Product Accounts (NIPA) do exactly this in Table 1.11, which expresses components of national income as a percentage of Gross Domestic Income.

Two series from that table tell the story directly:

Labor's share — compensation of employees (wages, salaries, benefits) as a percentage of GDI. This captures what workers receive for their labor out of total economic output.

Corporate profit share — after-tax corporate profits (with inventory valuation and capital consumption adjustments) as a percentage of GDI. This captures what corporations retain after paying taxes.

Because both use the same denominator, one rising and the other falling is a direct, apples-to-apples statement about the redistribution of economic output.

Three inflection points

The Moments That Shifted the Balance

The data shows not a smooth, gradual drift but a series of structural breaks — moments when the rules of the economy changed and the shares reset to a new level.

1980–83
The structural break

What happened: The Volcker Fed raised interest rates to nearly 20% to break inflation, triggering a severe recession. Reagan fired striking air traffic controllers in 1981, fundamentally altering the balance of power between employers and labor. Deregulation accelerated, the shareholder value movement took hold, and globalization began opening American labor markets to international competition.

What followed: Labor share fell from ~51% in 1970 to ~46% by the mid-1980s and never recovered to postwar levels. This was not a cyclical dip — it was a permanent reset. Corporate profits stabilized and began a long upward climb.

2008–09
The hinge

What happened: The financial crisis wiped out vast household wealth — primarily home equity. The federal government and Federal Reserve intervened aggressively to rescue the financial system through TARP, near-zero interest rates, and quantitative easing, which rapidly reflated asset prices.

What followed: Corporate profits recovered within two years and surged to historic highs, fueled by cheap borrowing, cost-cutting, and suppressed wage growth. Labor share fell to its lowest levels since World War II and stayed there through the entire subsequent expansion.

2020–21
The anomaly

What happened: The pandemic triggered the sharpest GDP contraction since the Depression. Massive government stimulus — direct payments, enhanced unemployment, PPP loans — temporarily inflated household income, nudging labor share upward. Many businesses shuttered or contracted sharply.

What followed: The apparent labor recovery evaporated almost immediately. As stimulus ended and supply chain disruptions enabled companies to raise prices faster than wages, corporate profit share surged to its highest level in the entire 76-year dataset — over 9% of GDI. The COVID spike in labor share was a transfer payment artifact, not a structural change.

"The post-2008 recovery was real in GDP terms — but the gains flowed almost entirely to the profit side. In previous expansions, labor share would stabilize or recover. After 2008, it just kept falling."

What the data does and doesn't show

An Honest Reading

The divergence shown here is real and well-documented. A few caveats are worth keeping in mind:

Causation is not established. The data shows that labor share has fallen as corporate profit share has risen. It does not prove that one caused the other. Both could reflect common underlying drivers — globalization, technology, industry concentration — rather than a direct transfer.

Labor compensation includes benefits. The labor share series captures total compensation including employer-paid healthcare and pension contributions, not just wages. Rising healthcare costs mean that some of the apparent stability in labor share through the 1990s reflects rising benefit costs, not rising take-home pay.

Corporate profits are volatile. The profit share series swings sharply with the business cycle — it collapsed in recessions and surged in recoveries. The trend is real, but individual years can be misleading.

The 2020s data may not be the final word. The post-COVID profit surge coincided with unusual supply chain dynamics and pricing power. Whether the profit share remains above 9% of GDI through a more normal economic environment remains to be seen.

Dual-axis charts can mislead. The main chart plots labor share on the left axis (40–55%) and profit share on the right axis (0–15%). Where the two lines appear to cross is an artifact of how those axes are anchored, not a meaningful data event. Labor compensation has always been far larger than corporate profits as a share of GDI — the lines never represent equal values. What the chart reliably shows is the direction each series moves over time, not the relationship between their visual positions.

None of these caveats change the central finding: over 76 years, and especially since 2008, the share of national economic output flowing to labor has declined while the share flowing to corporate profits has risen. That is what the data shows.
